1. Shoulder Mode — Live Evidence

Shoulder mode is the operating regime that results from QSH's aggregate demand gate. Every inverter-driven ASHP has a minimum modulation threshold — a load floor below which the compressor operates inefficiently or short-cycles. For the Cosy 6, this threshold is 2 kW (configurable per HP model in QSH's YAML configuration). QSH monitors total house demand continuously across all zones. When aggregate demand is below this threshold the HP does not run, regardless of individual zone calls. When demand aggregates above threshold — morning warm-up, evening peak, post-DHW recovery — the HP runs properly loaded. The 38–49°C flow temperatures observed in shoulder mode are the thermodynamic consequence of a correctly loaded HP operating in mild outdoor conditions, not a deliberate setpoint choice. Individual zones that call for heat while aggregate demand is below threshold are allowed to cool below setpoint; this is intentional behaviour. A zone that persistently drifts below setpoint while the HP is otherwise running (aggregate demand above threshold) is an indicator of a localised thermal leak in that zone — a diagnostic signal delivered without explicit fault detection logic.

2. Why Weather Compensation Cannot Achieve Shoulder Mode

Weather compensation has no concept of aggregate demand: WC runs the HP whenever any single zone calls for heat, regardless of whether total load exceeds the HP's minimum modulation threshold. At 9°C outdoor (shoulder mode average), a typical WC curve sets flow to approximately 29°C — and fires the HP even if total building demand is 0.8 kW. The compressor runs at low load, efficiency collapses, and short-cycling risk increases.

QSH gates on total house demand: The 2 kW minimum modulation threshold is a hard run condition. Below it, QSH holds the HP off. When aggregate demand rises above threshold the HP runs properly loaded, which in mild conditions (9°C outdoor, low fabric loss) means higher flow temperatures than WC would set — 38–49°C versus WC's ~29°C — because the HP is now absorbing sufficient load to operate efficiently. The COP uplift to 4.77 is the direct thermodynamic result of running the compressor in its efficient load band.

The fundamental difference: WC asks "what flow temperature should I run at?" QSH first asks "should I run at all?" Only when aggregate demand justifies operation does flow temperature selection apply. This sequencing — demand gate before setpoint calculation — is the mechanism WC cannot replicate with a single-variable outdoor-to-flow curve.

Modelling Methodology

Adjusted_COP = (1 − shoulder_frac) × QSH_COP + shoulder_frac × Shoulder_COP_arch

Energy_saving = WC_kWh × (1 − WC_COP / Adjusted_COP)
Scope of uplift ratio: The 1.219× uplift ratio is measured from a single Cosy 6 installation. The minimum-modulation efficiency principle applies universally to all inverter-driven ASHPs — every HP benefits from operating above its low-load inefficiency floor — but the magnitude of the COP uplift is a property of each HP model's compressor efficiency curve at different load points. Applicability of the 1.219× ratio to Daikin, Ecodan, Vaillant, Samsung, and Grant HP models is assumed, not validated. Per-model calibration requires operational data from each HP type under shoulder mode conditions. The direction of the effect (higher load = better COP in mild conditions) is universal; the size of the effect is Cosy 6-derived.

Conservative demand assumptions: The model uses the current 6% shoulder duty cycle as baseline. In practice, better-insulated archetypes (newbuild, retrofitted) carry higher thermal mass relative to their fabric loss, giving the demand gate more opportunities to accumulate above threshold in mild weather. Spring and autumn data will likely show higher shoulder duty fractions than this late-winter sample.

6. The Missing Trick — What the Fleet Model Doesn't Capture

Current Fleet Model Gap

The fleet digital twin models QSH's adaptive flow control and achieves a +1.8–2.7% COP advantage over WC across archetypes. This captures the benefit of demand-responsive flow temperature selection but does not model the discrete shoulder mode operating regime where COP jumps by 21.9%.

With shoulder mode modelled at even the current conservative 6% duty cycle, the fleet advantage over WC doubles to +3.1–4.0%. At a realistic annual shoulder duty of 15–20% (spring/autumn seasons), the advantage grows to +5.9–7.1%.

7. Impact Claim

If shoulder mode were incorporated into the fleet digital twin, the modelled QSH advantage over weather compensation would increase from +1.8–2.7% COP to +3.1–7.1% COP (depending on shoulder duty cycle assumptions).

This is supported by 796 real-world shoulder mode data points showing COP 4.77 versus 3.91 during normal heating — a 21.9% uplift that is thermodynamically consistent (lower flow temp → higher HP COP) and operationally validated (the improvement was achieved through improved shoulder mode detection, not measurement artefact).

For a typical UK semi-detached home, this translates to an additional 90–185 kWh/yr electricity saving (£22–£45/yr at current rates) beyond what the current fleet model predicts. For larger properties like the 205 m² reference installation, savings scale proportionally higher.

Limitations: The shoulder mode data covers 16 days of late-winter operation (Feb–Mar). Spring/autumn data will likely show higher shoulder duty cycles. The 20% duty projection is an estimate — a full heating season of data is needed to validate the annual shoulder fraction. The COP uplift ratio (1.219×) is measured from a single Cosy 6 installation; the minimum-modulation efficiency principle is universal to inverter-driven ASHPs, but the magnitude of the uplift is HP-model specific and requires per-model validation. Fleet impact projections applying 1.219× uniformly across all 8 archetypes should be treated as indicative of direction and order of magnitude, not as per-model predictions.
